# The Action Map

**Becoming the Engineer Big Companies Hire — A Comprehensive Self-Assessment for the .NET Backend Engineer**

_Derived from: The Software Engineer (reference document)_

---

This document takes every pillar from the reference document and translates it into concrete, honest action. Its purpose is two things simultaneously: to show you what you already have, and to name exactly what is missing. Neither flattery nor pessimism — just an accurate map of the distance between where you are and where the job market, specifically the top tier of it, expects you to be.

Read it once in full. Then return to each domain and work through it.

---

## Your Starting Point

Before working domain by domain, it helps to be honest about the baseline. Two years of production .NET experience as a freelance backend engineer, a graduation project with real distributed components, and hands-on LLM integration in production places you ahead of the average final-year student by a significant margin. You are not starting from zero.

What you are missing is not talent or motivation. What is missing is systematic coverage — the deliberate filling of gaps that freelance work does not force you to confront. Freelance projects solve client problems. Big companies want engineers who can reason about systems at scale, perform under structured interview conditions, and demonstrate judgment across a wide surface area of engineering knowledge.

This document names those gaps without softening them.

## Domain 1: C# and .NET Language Mastery

### What Big Companies Expect

A senior candidate at a large company is expected to know their primary language not just at the usage level but at the behavioral level. For .NET specifically, this means understanding what the runtime is doing, not just what the code says. Interviewers will ask you to reason about performance, memory, and concurrency without looking anything up. They expect you to have opinions about language design decisions and know why those decisions were made.

### Where You Currently Stand

You have two years writing production C# across real client projects and a graduation project with ASP.NET Core 8, Clean Architecture, Hangfire, JWT, and FCM integration. This means you are productive and experienced. What is less certain is whether you have gone below the surface of the language — the runtime internals, memory model, and advanced concurrency patterns that distinguish a competent user from a deep practitioner.

**What you likely have:**

- Solid command of C# syntax, LINQ, generics, and async/await at a usage level
- Practical knowledge of ASP.NET Core: middleware, dependency injection, routing, filters
- Real experience with Clean Architecture layering and separation of concerns
- Background job patterns via Hangfire
- JWT authentication and claims-based authorization in production

**What is likely missing or shallow:**

- CLR internals: how the garbage collector works across generations (Gen0, Gen1, Gen2, LOH), what triggers a collection, and what that means for your allocations
- Value types vs. reference types at the memory level — boxing, unboxing, struct layout, why this matters for performance
- `Span<T>` and `Memory<T>` — the modern way to work with memory in .NET without allocating; most engineers using older versions do not know these
- Advanced async patterns: `IAsyncEnumerable<T>`, `System.Threading.Channels`, `ValueTask` vs. `Task` and when the difference matters
- The `IDisposable` pattern and `IAsyncDisposable` — not just implementing them, but knowing what actually happens with unmanaged resources and when finalization is involved
- Modern C# features beyond version 8: records, init-only properties, pattern matching depth, required members, primary constructors
- Reflection and source generators — what they are, when to use each, and the performance implications of reflection at runtime
- The difference between `lock`, `Monitor`, `SemaphoreSlim`, `ReaderWriterLockSlim`, `Interlocked`, and `volatile` — and when each is appropriate
- BenchmarkDotNet — how to measure performance honestly, not by intuition

### What to Study

**CLR Internals:**

- _CLR via C#_ by Jeffrey Richter — the single most important book for deep .NET knowledge. Chapters on memory management, type system, and threading are essential.
- Microsoft's documentation on the .NET garbage collector internals — read it, do not skim it.

**Modern C# Language:**

- _C# in Depth_ by Jon Skeet (5th edition covers C# 9 and 10) — the most thorough treatment of the language itself.
- The official C# language specification for features you use daily but have not read the specification for.

**Performance:**

- _Pro .NET Memory Management_ by Konrad Kokosa — specific to .NET memory, allocations, and GC behavior.
- Stephen Toub's blog posts on async/await — he wrote the implementation; reading his explanations is the closest thing to primary source documentation.

**Concurrency:**

- _Concurrency in C# Cookbook_ by Stephen Cleary — practical patterns for async, parallel, and concurrent code.

### What to Build and Practice

- Take a real piece of code from one of your freelance projects and profile it with BenchmarkDotNet. Find the allocation hotspots. Fix them using `Span<T>` or `ArrayPool<T>`. Measure the difference.
- Implement a thread-safe in-memory cache from scratch using `ReaderWriterLockSlim`. Do not reach for `ConcurrentDictionary` until you understand what it is hiding.
- Build a producer-consumer pipeline using `System.Threading.Channels`. This pattern appears in high-throughput systems and interviewers at backend-heavy companies will recognize it.
- Write a set of unit tests that deliberately trigger GC pressure and observe the behavior with dotMemory or the built-in diagnostics tools.

### Self-Check Questions

- Can you explain what happens when an object crosses from Gen0 to Gen2 in the .NET garbage collector, and why that matters for latency-sensitive applications?
- What is the difference between `Task` and `ValueTask`, and when does the allocation overhead of `Task` actually matter?
- If you have a high-frequency loop allocating small objects, what are your options for reducing GC pressure in .NET?
- What does `volatile` guarantee, and what does it not guarantee?

## Domain 2: Data Structures and Algorithms

### What Big Companies Expect

This is the domain where big companies most sharply filter candidates, and it is the domain most disconnected from day-to-day backend work. Regardless of how experienced you are in production, a first-round coding interview at Microsoft, Google, Amazon, or any large tech company will test your ability to solve algorithmic problems under time pressure and explain your reasoning out loud while doing it.

The standard is not that you memorize solutions. It is that you have internalized patterns well enough that you can recognize which pattern applies to an unfamiliar problem and then implement it correctly.

### Where You Currently Stand

University coursework gave you the foundations. Two years of production work gave you experience, but almost certainly not the kind of systematic, pattern-focused algorithmic practice that interview preparation requires. Production code rarely asks you to find the longest palindromic substring. The gap here is not intelligence — it is deliberate practice of a specific format.

**What you likely have:**

- Foundational knowledge of common data structures from university
- Ability to reason about time complexity at a basic level
- Practical use of .NET collections: `Dictionary<TKey,TValue>`, `List<T>`, `Queue<T>`, `Stack<T>`, `HashSet<T>`

**What is likely missing:**

- Interview-ready speed and confidence across the core problem patterns
- Deep practice with graph algorithms: BFS, DFS, Dijkstra, topological sort, union-find
- Dynamic programming — recognizing when a problem has optimal substructure and how to formulate the recurrence
- Binary search beyond the trivial case — applying binary search to problems where the search space is not an array
- Tree problems: LCA, diameter, serialize/deserialize, path sums
- Heap-based problems: streaming medians, k-closest points, merge k sorted lists
- Two-pointer and sliding window patterns applied to non-obvious problems
- Backtracking — permutations, combinations, N-Queens, Sudoku solver
- `PriorityQueue<TElement,TPriority>` in .NET 6+ — most .NET engineers do not know it was added or how to use it correctly

### What to Study

**Primary resource:**

- _Blind 75_ problem list — the curated set of 75 problems that covers every pattern. Solve each problem in C#. Do not move to the next until you can solve the current one within 20 minutes without reference.

**Supplementary:**

- _Neetcode 150_ — an expanded and well-organized set with video explanations organized by pattern. The pattern grouping is more useful than random practice.
- _Cracking the Coding Interview_ by Gayle Laakmann McDowell — for interview strategy, communication, and the walkthrough method, not just the problems.

**Complexity analysis:**

- Practice deriving time and space complexity for every solution you write. Do not accept "it works" as the endpoint.

### What to Build and Practice

- Solve two to three LeetCode problems per day, organized by pattern. Do not mix patterns randomly.
- Follow this pattern sequence: Arrays and Hashing → Two Pointers → Sliding Window → Stack → Binary Search → Linked List → Trees → Tries → Heap/Priority Queue → Intervals → Greedy → Graphs → Dynamic Programming (1D then 2D) → Backtracking.
- For each problem you solve, write the solution in C# using the most idiomatic .NET collections. This reinforces language knowledge alongside algorithmic thinking.
- Practice talking through problems out loud before writing code. Interviewers evaluate your reasoning process, not just the output.
- After solving a problem, always look at the optimal solution and understand why it is optimal, even if your solution passed.

### Self-Check Questions

- Can you implement BFS and DFS from memory on a graph represented as an adjacency list?
- Can you solve a basic dynamic programming problem (like Longest Common Subsequence) by first identifying the subproblems, writing the recurrence relation, and then coding either top-down or bottom-up?
- Can you implement binary search on a rotated sorted array without bugs on the first attempt?
- Can you explain the difference between a min-heap and a max-heap, and implement one using `PriorityQueue<TElement,TPriority>`?

## Domain 3: System Design and Architecture

### What Big Companies Expect

For mid-level and senior roles, the system design interview is often the highest-signal conversation. The interviewer gives you a vague prompt — "Design Twitter," "Design a URL shortener," "Design a notification service" — and expects you to drive the conversation, ask clarifying questions, make scoped decisions, identify tradeoffs, and arrive at a coherent architecture within 45 minutes.

They are not looking for the perfect answer. They are looking for structured thinking, honest acknowledgment of tradeoffs, and the ability to reason about scale, failure, and consistency without prompting.

**The specific expectations:**

- You know how to estimate scale: QPS, storage needs, bandwidth requirements
- You know the distributed systems primitives: consistent hashing, leader election, consensus
- You understand database selection: when to use SQL, when NoSQL, when to use both
- You can design for failure: what happens if a service goes down, a network partition occurs, a database becomes unavailable
- You can reason about consistency: strong, eventual, causal — and which the system actually needs
- You have a repertoire of design patterns for common problems: rate limiting, feed generation, search, notifications, file storage

### Where You Currently Stand

Your Clean Architecture implementation in InterVision shows you think in layers and boundaries. You have real experience with the components a backend system needs. What is almost certainly missing is the scale dimension — the habit of thinking about a system that serves millions, not hundreds, of users — and the structured interview format of system design.

**What you likely have:**

- Clean Architecture layering: entities, use cases, interfaces, infrastructure
- Practical understanding of REST API design from production work
- Background job processing patterns (Hangfire)
- Object storage integration (Cloudflare R2)
- Basic database design with SQL Server
- JWT-based authentication flows

**What is likely missing:**

- Systematic knowledge of distributed systems primitives: CAP theorem applied to real decisions, consistency models, consensus algorithms at a conceptual level
- Caching architecture: Redis as a caching layer, cache-aside vs. write-through vs. write-behind, cache invalidation strategies, TTL design
- Message brokers: RabbitMQ or Azure Service Bus — not just knowing they exist, but understanding delivery guarantees, dead letter queues, consumer groups, backpressure
- Microservices patterns: API gateway, service discovery, circuit breaker (Polly in .NET), sidecar pattern
- Database sharding and replication: read replicas, connection pooling at scale, CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation) as an architectural pattern, not just a concept
- Event sourcing: what it is, when it is appropriate, and why it is often over-applied
- CDN, load balancer, and DNS behavior — how a request actually travels from a client to your server
- Rate limiting patterns: token bucket, leaky bucket, fixed window, sliding window — and how to implement them in a distributed system

### What to Study

**Books:**

- _Designing Data-Intensive Applications_ by Martin Kleppmann — this is the most important book for system design. Read it cover to cover, not just the famous chapters. Pay particular attention to chapters on replication, partitioning, transactions, and consensus.
- _System Design Interview Vol. 1 and Vol. 2_ by Alex Xu — structured walkthroughs of the interview format. Use as practice reference, not as substitutes for understanding.

**.NET-specific architectural resources:**

- Microsoft's eShopOnContainers reference application — a real, complex microservices application built with .NET. Reading the source code is more valuable than most blog posts.
- The Microsoft Architecture Center documentation on CQRS, event sourcing, and microservices patterns in .NET.

**Specific patterns to master in .NET:**

- MediatR for CQRS implementation — you may already know it; if not, learn it
- Polly for resilience patterns: retry, circuit breaker, timeout, bulkhead
- Refit or HttpClientFactory for resilient HTTP client patterns
- StackExchange.Redis for Redis integration in .NET

### What to Build and Practice

- Design one classic system per week without reference. Set a timer for 45 minutes. Write out: clarifying questions, scale estimation, high-level design, component deep dives, failure scenarios. Then compare your design to a reference solution.
- Integrate Redis into InterVision or a new project. Implement cache-aside for expensive queries. Observe the latency difference with structured logging.
- Set up RabbitMQ locally and build a simple publish-subscribe system in .NET. Implement dead letter queue handling. Understand what happens when a consumer crashes mid-processing.
- Add Polly to a project. Configure retry with exponential backoff and a circuit breaker. Test the circuit breaker by killing the dependency.
- Practice the CQRS pattern explicitly: separate your read models from your write models. Notice where this simplifies your code and where it introduces overhead.

### Self-Check Questions

- If your database becomes a bottleneck at scale, what are your options in order of increasing complexity, and what does each cost?
- What is the difference between eventual consistency and strong consistency, and can you give a real example where choosing eventual consistency would be the correct engineering decision?
- How does consistent hashing solve the problem of data redistribution when a node is added to or removed from a cluster?
- Design a rate limiter for an API that must work correctly even when deployed across 20 instances behind a load balancer.

## Domain 4: Databases — SQL, ORM, and Beyond

### What Big Companies Expect

Databases are where most application performance problems live. Big companies expect backend engineers to understand not just how to write queries, but how the database executes them — and how to change the execution plan when it chooses poorly. They expect engineers to know the difference between a clustered and a non-clustered index without hesitation, to identify an N+1 query problem in an EF Core trace, and to make informed decisions about when to denormalize.

### Where You Currently Stand

SQL Server and EF Core in production gives you real experience. The question is depth. Production work teaches you what works; it rarely forces you to understand why it works or what the cost is.

**What you likely have:**

- SQL Server schema design and basic query writing
- EF Core: migrations, relationships, basic query patterns
- Understanding of primary keys, foreign keys, and basic indexing

**What is likely missing:**

- Advanced SQL: window functions (`ROW_NUMBER()`, `RANK()`, `LEAD()`, `LAG()`, `SUM() OVER(...)`), CTEs (including recursive CTEs), `MERGE`, `EXCEPT`, `INTERSECT`
- Query execution plans: reading them in SQL Server Management Studio, understanding Scan vs. Seek, Nested Loops vs. Hash Match vs. Merge Join, and when each is appropriate
- Index design: clustered vs. non-clustered, covering indexes, included columns, filtered indexes, when indexing hurts (writes) vs. helps (reads)
- Transaction isolation levels: Read Uncommitted, Read Committed, Repeatable Read, Serializable, and Snapshot — what each prevents and what anomaly each allows
- EF Core internals: the N+1 problem and how to detect it, eager vs. lazy vs. explicit loading, query compilation, `AsNoTracking()` and when it matters, raw SQL with EF when the ORM is working against you
- Redis: not just as a concept but hands-on — Redis data structures (strings, hashes, lists, sets, sorted sets), pub/sub, transactions (`MULTI`/`EXEC`), Lua scripting, expiry policies
- Understanding when to reach for a document store (MongoDB) or a search engine (Elasticsearch/OpenSearch) and what you give up by doing so

### What to Study

- _Use The Index, Luke_ (free at use-the-index-luke.com) — the clearest explanation of database indexing and query optimization across SQL databases. Read it fully.
- The SQL Server query execution plan documentation — learn to read actual execution plans in SSMS, not estimated ones.
- EF Core documentation on performance — Microsoft has a dedicated performance section that covers the pitfalls explicitly.
- Redis documentation — the official docs are excellent. Read the data types section and the transactions section.

### What to Build and Practice

- Take a SQL Server database from one of your freelance projects and run `SET STATISTICS IO ON` and `SET STATISTICS TIME ON` before your most common queries. Read the output. Find the most expensive query. Look at its execution plan. Add an index if appropriate. Measure the improvement.
- Find a place in your existing code where EF Core is producing an N+1 query. Fix it. Measure the before and after with SQL Server Profiler or EF Core logging.
- Write ten window function queries against a dataset you care about. Window functions appear in almost every analytics or reporting requirement and most engineers avoid them because they feel unfamiliar.
- Set up Redis locally. Implement a distributed rate limiter using Redis sorted sets. This is a realistic interview exercise that also teaches you Redis data structures practically.
- Implement the outbox pattern — writing domain events to a database table in the same transaction as the business operation, then publishing them asynchronously — using EF Core. This pattern solves a real distributed systems problem and demonstrates understanding of transactions.

### Self-Check Questions

- What is the difference between a clustered and non-clustered index in SQL Server, and what happens when a non-clustered index does not cover all the columns in a query?
- If EF Core is generating 101 queries for a list of 100 objects with a related collection, what caused it and what are your options to fix it?
- What does `READ COMMITTED SNAPSHOT ISOLATION` give you in SQL Server that standard `READ COMMITTED` does not?
- When would you choose Redis over an in-memory cache within a single application process, and what do you give up by choosing Redis?

## Domain 5: Production Engineering

### What Big Companies Expect

At a big company, the production environment is the real system. Engineers are expected to know how to deploy their code, observe its behavior, and respond to problems — not just hand it off to a DevOps team. The expectation is that you have thought about how your code runs in production, not just whether it compiles.

Specific expectations include: you know what Docker is and can write a Dockerfile for your application, you understand what a CI/CD pipeline does and can configure one, you know how to write structured logs and why unstructured logs are a liability at scale, and you have at least a working understanding of a major cloud provider's core services.

### Where You Currently Stand

You have production experience, which means you have shipped and operated real systems. Cloudflare R2 and Hangfire show some production thinking. However, the specific skills — containerization, CI/CD, structured observability, cloud-native deployment — are likely missing or thin.

**What you likely have:**

- Real production systems that users depend on
- Background job operations and monitoring at a basic level
- Object storage integration (R2)
- Experience with JWT lifecycle management in production

**What is likely missing:**

- Docker: writing a Dockerfile for an ASP.NET Core application, multi-stage builds to keep images small, Docker Compose for local development with dependencies
- Container orchestration: Kubernetes at a conceptual level — pods, services, deployments, config maps, secrets, horizontal scaling, health probes. You do not need to be a Kubernetes expert, but you need to understand the concepts.
- CI/CD: GitHub Actions is the most accessible starting point. A pipeline that builds, tests, lints, and deploys your application automatically. This is expected at any serious company.
- Structured logging: Serilog with structured output to a sink (Seq locally, or an Elasticsearch/Loki sink in production). Not `Console.WriteLine`. Logs that carry context — request ID, user ID, correlation ID — and can be queried.
- OpenTelemetry: the emerging standard for distributed tracing across the .NET ecosystem. Activity API, trace context propagation, exporting to Jaeger or Zipkin.
- Health checks: ASP.NET Core's built-in health check middleware — readiness vs. liveness, checking database connectivity, external dependencies
- Azure: since .NET runs heavily on Azure, familiarity with the core services is expected in many companies — App Service, Azure SQL, Azure Service Bus, Azure Blob Storage, Azure Container Registry, Application Insights

### What to Study

- Docker's official getting-started documentation — thorough and free. Supplement with the official Microsoft documentation on containerizing ASP.NET Core applications.
- GitHub Actions documentation — start with the quickstart for .NET.
- Serilog documentation and the structured logging section of the Microsoft .NET logging documentation.
- OpenTelemetry .NET documentation.
- Microsoft Azure Fundamentals (AZ-900) — free study material, and the certification itself signals cloud literacy to employers. Not mandatory, but high signal for low effort.

### What to Build and Practice

- Containerize InterVision. Write a Dockerfile that produces a minimal production image using multi-stage builds. Verify it runs identically to the local development environment.
- Write a GitHub Actions workflow for a .NET project that: restores packages, builds, runs tests, and reports results. Add a step that only deploys if the tests pass.
- Add Serilog to an existing project. Configure it to write structured JSON logs. Add a correlation ID middleware that stamps every log entry with the request ID. Then look at the logs for a real request and see if you can trace it from entry to exit.
- Implement health checks for an ASP.NET Core application: one that checks database connectivity, one that checks an external HTTP dependency. Configure separate `/health/live` and `/health/ready` endpoints.
- Set up Application Insights locally (it can run with just an instrumentation key pointed at Azure's free tier) and observe your application's requests, dependencies, and exceptions as a unified trace.

### Self-Check Questions

- What is the difference between a Docker image and a Docker container, and why do multi-stage builds produce smaller images?
- What is the difference between a readiness probe and a liveness probe in Kubernetes, and what happens when each one fails?
- Why is `Console.WriteLine` insufficient for production logging, and what does structured logging give you that it does not?
- What is a correlation ID, and why does it matter in a system with multiple services?

## Domain 6: Testing as Engineering Practice

### What Big Companies Expect

At a large company, code without tests is considered incomplete. Not as a policy but as a professional standard. Engineers are expected to write unit tests for their domain logic, integration tests for their infrastructure layer, and to understand the tradeoffs between test types. They are also expected to know what makes a test trustworthy — deterministic, isolated, fast — and what makes it a liability.

### Where You Currently Stand

This is likely the area where the gap between your freelance experience and large company expectations is widest. Freelance projects are time-pressured and client-driven; testing is often the first thing sacrificed. This is rational in context, but it means you may not have deeply internalized the testing habits that large companies treat as baseline.

**What you likely have:**

- Basic knowledge of unit testing from university
- Some ad-hoc manual testing in production

**What is likely missing:**

- Systematic unit testing practice: xUnit as the .NET standard, `[Fact]` and `[Theory]`, test naming conventions that describe behavior not implementation
- Mocking: Moq or NSubstitute — faking dependencies so you can test a unit in isolation
- Integration testing with `WebApplicationFactory<TProgram>` — spinning up your actual ASP.NET Core application in a test context and making real HTTP calls against it
- TestContainers for .NET — spinning up a real SQL Server or Redis instance in Docker for integration tests, rather than faking the database
- Test architecture: the Arrange-Act-Assert pattern, the one assertion per test principle, the difference between a stub and a mock and a fake
- Test-Driven Development as a practice: writing the test before the implementation, and understanding what that discipline does to your API design

### What to Study

- _The Art of Unit Testing_ by Roy Osherove — the clearest, most practical treatment of unit testing principles. The .NET examples are directly applicable.
- xUnit documentation and the Microsoft testing documentation for ASP.NET Core.
- TestContainers for .NET GitHub repository and documentation.
- NSubstitute documentation — it tends to produce cleaner test code than Moq for most scenarios.

### What to Build and Practice

- Take the domain layer of InterVision and write a full suite of unit tests for it. Every use case handler should have tests for its happy path, its validation errors, and its edge cases. Aim for 80% coverage of the domain layer as a minimum.
- Write an integration test for one of your API endpoints using `WebApplicationFactory<TProgram>`. Use a real test database (either an in-memory database for simple cases, or TestContainers for realistic ones). Test the full request-response cycle.
- Practice TDD for the next feature you implement, regardless of how small. Write the test first. Watch it fail. Write the minimum code to make it pass. Refactor. Notice how this changes the design of your API.
- Find a bug in a current or past project. Before fixing it, write a test that reproduces the bug. Fix the bug. Confirm the test passes. This practice — writing a failing test before fixing a bug — is one of the most effective habits in software engineering.

### Self-Check Questions

- What is the difference between a unit test and an integration test, and why does the distinction matter for test suite design?
- What does it mean for a test to be deterministic, and what are the most common sources of non-determinism in tests?
- When should you use a mock versus a stub versus a fake, and what problem is each solving?
- What does `WebApplicationFactory<TProgram>` give you, and when is a real integration test more valuable than a unit test with mocks?

## Domain 7: AI Engineering

### What Big Companies Expect

AI engineering is no longer a specialty track — it is becoming a baseline expectation at many companies, particularly those building user-facing products. The expectation is not that you can train models; it is that you can integrate them reliably, design systems that behave predictably despite non-deterministic components, and evaluate whether your AI features are actually working.

### Where You Currently Stand

This is one of your genuine differentiators. Production LLM integration with Groq and Gemini, AI-driven background jobs in InterVision — you have real experience that most candidates graduating in 2026 do not have. The question is whether you have gone deep enough to design serious AI systems, or whether your current experience is primarily at the API call level.

**What you likely have:**

- Production LLM API integration (Groq, Gemini)
- Async AI pipelines using Hangfire
- Real understanding of latency and error handling for LLM calls

**What is likely missing:**

- Semantic Kernel — Microsoft's official .NET AI orchestration framework. It is becoming the standard for .NET AI applications and companies building on Azure OpenAI will expect familiarity with it.
- RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) architecture: vector databases, embedding generation, chunking strategies, reranking, hybrid search. You know the concept; you need to have built it.
- Agent patterns: tool use, planning loops, multi-step reasoning, memory management across turns
- Prompt engineering as a discipline: system prompts, few-shot examples, chain-of-thought, structured output with JSON mode
- Evaluation pipelines: how do you know your AI feature is working? Automated evaluation using an LLM as a judge, golden dataset testing, regression detection
- Cost management: token counting, caching LLM responses, batching, model selection based on task complexity
- Vector databases: Qdrant, Weaviate, or pgvector — understanding how similarity search works, HNSW indexing, and the tradeoffs between different vector database options

### What to Study

- Semantic Kernel documentation and the official Microsoft samples repository — start with the kernel concepts, then move to plugins and planners.
- The LangChain conceptual documentation (even as a .NET engineer) — it is the most comprehensive treatment of AI application patterns and the concepts transfer directly.
- Andrej Karpathy's lectures on language models — understanding how LLMs work internally makes you a better integrator of them.
- The Qdrant documentation and the pgvector extension documentation for PostgreSQL.

### What to Build and Practice

- Build a RAG pipeline from scratch in .NET: take a set of documents, chunk them, generate embeddings using a model (OpenAI's `text-embedding-3-small` or a local model via Ollama), store them in Qdrant or pgvector, and build a retrieval + generation pipeline that answers questions about the documents. This is the single most valuable AI project you can have on your portfolio right now.
- Add an evaluation step to the InterVision AI pipeline: after generating a session insight, use a separate LLM call to evaluate the quality of the insight against a rubric. Log the score. This is how production AI systems are monitored.
- Integrate Semantic Kernel into a new or existing project. Use its plugin system to give the model access to a tool (a database query, an API call). Observe how the model selects tools and what happens when tool calls fail.
- Build a simple agent that can answer multi-step questions by using tools iteratively. This forces you to think about planning loops, tool result handling, and context management.

### Self-Check Questions

- What is the difference between semantic search and keyword search, and why does RAG need both to work reliably in production?
- What happens to the quality of a RAG system when your chunks are too large? Too small?
- What is a Semantic Kernel plugin, and how does it differ from a raw tool call to an LLM API?
- How would you detect degradation in the quality of an LLM-powered feature in production without human review of every response?

## Domain 8: Security

### What Big Companies Expect

Security is not optional and is not a separate team's responsibility. Big companies expect backend engineers to be fluent in authentication and authorization patterns, to know the OWASP Top 10 by name and understand the vulnerability each represents, and to handle secrets, tokens, and credentials as a matter of professional hygiene.

### Where You Currently Stand

JWT implementation in production gives you real authentication experience. The breadth of security knowledge — particularly around common vulnerabilities and secrets management — is likely thinner.

**What you likely have:**

- JWT generation, validation, and claims-based authorization in ASP.NET Core
- Basic input handling in API controllers
- HTTPS awareness

**What is likely missing:**

- OWASP Top 10 fluency: Injection, Broken Authentication, Sensitive Data Exposure, XML External Entities, Broken Access Control, Security Misconfiguration, Cross-Site Scripting, Insecure Deserialization, Using Components with Known Vulnerabilities, Insufficient Logging and Monitoring — know each one, know how it is exploited, and know how to prevent it in .NET
- Refresh token rotation and the security implications of long-lived tokens
- Secrets management: Azure Key Vault or HashiCorp Vault for storing secrets, not environment variables or `appsettings.json`
- IDOR (Insecure Direct Object Reference) prevention — one of the most common and most underappreciated vulnerabilities in API design
- SQL injection in the context of EF Core — when is EF Core protecting you and when can you still be vulnerable (raw SQL, string interpolation in `FromSqlRaw`)
- Role-based vs. policy-based authorization in ASP.NET Core — the difference and when each is appropriate
- Rate limiting as a security control, not just a reliability control
- CORS configuration — what it is protecting, what it is not protecting, and why a wildcard CORS policy is a real risk

### What to Study

- The OWASP Top 10 documentation — free, authoritative, and readable. Read every item.
- Microsoft's ASP.NET Core security documentation — covers authentication, authorization, data protection, and anti-forgery in detail.
- Troy Hunt's blog — practical, industry-level security thinking from someone who has seen what actually happens when systems fail.

### What to Practice

- Audit one of your existing projects against the OWASP Top 10. For each item, determine whether your system is vulnerable, mitigated, or not applicable. Document your findings.
- Implement Azure Key Vault or a `.env` file manager for local development — remove all secrets from `appsettings.json` and environment variables where they might be committed accidentally.
- Add ASP.NET Core's built-in rate limiting middleware (available from .NET 7+) to a project. Configure both a global rate limit and a per-endpoint rate limit. Test what happens when a client exceeds it.
- Write an authorization policy in ASP.NET Core that goes beyond role checks — for example, a policy that verifies the requesting user owns the resource they are accessing. This is IDOR prevention at the framework level.

## Domain 9: Engineering Thinking and Judgment

### What Big Companies Expect

This domain cannot be studied directly. It develops through deliberate reflection on every decision you make. Big companies assess it indirectly — through how you explain past decisions in behavioral interviews, through how you navigate ambiguous system design prompts, and through how you respond when your assumptions are challenged.

The key behaviors: you ask clarifying questions before designing, you name tradeoffs explicitly rather than presenting a single answer as obviously correct, you can reason about failure modes before they occur, and you change your mind when given new information without becoming defensive.

### What to Practice

- Write Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) for InterVision. For every non-trivial technical decision you made, write a short document: the context, the options considered, the decision made, and the consequences. This practice builds the habit of explicit reasoning and produces artifacts you can show in interviews.
- When you encounter any engineering decision — which library to use, how to structure a query, whether to normalize a schema — force yourself to name at least two alternatives and one tradeoff for each before choosing. Do this in writing, not just in your head.
- Read post-mortems. Stripe, Cloudflare, GitHub, Atlassian, and others publish post-mortems of real production incidents. Reading them builds an intuition for failure modes you have not experienced personally.
- Develop a debugging protocol and follow it consistently. When something breaks: state what the expected behavior is, state what the observed behavior is, list what would have to be true for the observed behavior to occur, test one hypothesis at a time. Document the process, at least internally.

## Domain 10: The Interview Dimension

### Structure of a Big Company Interview Process

Large technology companies typically run a multi-stage process. Understanding the format prevents surprises.

**Stage 1 — Online Assessment or Phone Screen:** One or two algorithmic problems solved on a coding platform (HackerRank, LeetCode, CoderPad) within a time limit. Some companies also add a multiple-choice section on language or CS fundamentals.

**Stage 2 — Technical Phone Screen:** One or two coding problems solved while sharing your screen and talking through your approach. The interviewer is evaluating your reasoning as much as your code.

**Stage 3 — Virtual Onsite (4–6 rounds):** Typically includes two to three coding rounds, one system design round, one behavioral round, and sometimes a domain-specific round (e.g., backend architecture, database design).

### Coding Interview Preparation

The coding interview is a separate skill from production engineering. It must be practiced as its own discipline.

**The communication protocol interviewers expect:**

- Restate the problem in your own words before solving it
- Clarify constraints: input size, edge cases, expected output for edge inputs
- State your approach before writing code — "I am going to use a sliding window because..."
- Talk through your implementation as you write it
- Analyze time and space complexity before the interviewer asks
- Test your solution with at least one example and one edge case

**C# in coding interviews:** Most interviewers at large companies are comfortable with C#. Use it confidently. Know the standard library collections well enough to choose the right one without hesitation. Prefer readability over cleverness.

### System Design Interview Preparation

**The structured approach:**

1. Clarify requirements: functional (what the system does) and non-functional (scale, latency, availability)
2. Estimate scale: daily active users, requests per second, storage requirements, bandwidth
3. High-level design: draw the major components and their interactions
4. Deep dive: the interviewer will push on one or two components — go deep on those
5. Identify bottlenecks and address them
6. Discuss failure scenarios and how the system handles them

**Practice systems to design from memory:**

- URL shortener
- Rate limiter
- Notification service
- Chat system (WhatsApp-scale)
- Video upload and streaming service
- Search autocomplete
- Distributed cache
- News feed (Twitter/Instagram-scale)
- Ride-sharing location service
- Payment processing system

### Behavioral Interview Preparation

Large companies — especially Amazon — use structured behavioral interviews based on leadership principles. The STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the expected format.

**Stories you must prepare:**

- A time you made a technical decision that turned out to be wrong, and what you did about it
- A time you disagreed with a teammate or manager on a technical approach, and how you resolved it
- A time you had to deliver something under significant time or resource constraints
- A time you identified a problem that no one else had noticed
- A time you had to learn something new quickly in order to solve a problem
- A time you took ownership of something beyond your defined responsibilities
- A time you improved a process, codebase, or system significantly

**For each story:** be specific (name the project, the technology, the outcome), quantify the result where possible, and be honest about what you would do differently.

From your background, your stories should draw from InterVision, your freelance projects, and your AI integration work. The LLM pipeline debugging experience, the Clean Architecture decisions in InterVision, and managing client relationships as a freelancer are all legitimate interview material.

## Domain 11: Portfolio and Proof of Work

### What Big Companies Look For

A portfolio is not just a website. It is the body of evidence that demonstrates your claims are real. Every item on your CV should have a corresponding artifact: a GitHub repository, a deployed application, a technical article, or a recorded demo.

**What carries weight:**

- A GitHub profile with consistent commit history that shows sustained engineering work, not a burst of activity before applying
- Projects with READMEs that explain the architecture, the technical decisions, and the tradeoffs — not just installation instructions
- Technical writing that demonstrates depth: a blog post that goes beyond "here is how to do X" and explains when X is appropriate and when it is not
- Evidence of ownership: a project that has users, handles real traffic, or solves a real problem

**What does not carry weight:**

- Tutorial projects presented as original work
- Repositories with a single commit dated the week before an application deadline
- Claims on a CV with no supporting code, article, or reference

### Your Current Assets

- InterVision: a real graduation project with genuine complexity. The backend is yours to own completely.
- Two years of freelance projects: these exist but are often client-owned and cannot be shown publicly
- GitHub engineering knowledge base: demonstrates learning discipline and a habit of building artifacts
- LLM integration in production: a genuine differentiator in the current market

### What to Build Before Applying

- Write a detailed README that covers the architecture (include a diagram), the technical decisions (with reasoning), the components (Hangfire, R2, Groq, FCM), and the challenges solved
